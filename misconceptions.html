9 Historical Myths That Most People Still Believe

From Napoleon's height to the shape of medieval cosmology: these myths are wrong, well-documented as wrong, and somehow still in circulation.

By History Challenger·10 min read·April 2026

History is full of stories that feel true, get repeated in schools and films, and turn out to be fabrications, distortions, or misunderstandings. What follows are nine of the most persistent, examined against the actual historical record. None of these are fringe revisionism. All are mainstream scholarly consensus.

№ 01

Napoleon Bonaparte was unusually short

VERDICT: FALSE

Napoleon's supposed height of 5'2" derives from a unit confusion. His height was recorded as 5 pieds 2 pouces in French imperial units, which converts to roughly 5 feet 6 to 5 feet 7 inches in English measurement. This was about average for a French man of his era.

The myth was actively cultivated by British propagandists, particularly the caricaturist James Gillray, who depicted Napoleon as a tiny, raging figure to ridicule him. The image stuck. His nickname "le petit caporal" (the little corporal) was a term of affection from his troops, not a reference to his height.

His autopsy at St Helena, performed by his personal physician Francesco Antommarchi in 1821, recorded his height in French units as 5 pieds 2 pouces, which works out to roughly 5 feet 6 inches in English measurement. The confusion between French and English inches was noted by historians as early as the 19th century, but the myth proved more durable than the correction.

The record
Height recorded at the St Helena autopsy in 1821 by his personal physician Francesco Antommarchi: approximately 5 feet 6 inches in English measurement. Sources: Antommarchi's post-mortem notes (1821); historical analysis by Ben Weider and Sten Forshufvud.
№ 02

Columbus set out to prove the Earth was round

VERDICT: FALSE

Educated Europeans had known the Earth was spherical since at least the 3rd century BC, when Eratosthenes calculated its circumference with remarkable accuracy. By 1492, the spherical Earth was not a matter of debate among scholars, navigators, or the Spanish court that funded Columbus.

The actual dispute was about the size of the Earth, and therefore the distance to Asia by sailing west. Columbus's critics were not flat-Earthers. They correctly calculated that Asia was much farther away than Columbus claimed. Columbus was wrong about the distance; he survived only because an unexpected continent was in the way.

The flat-Earth myth about Columbus was largely invented by Washington Irving in his 1828 fictional biography A History of the Life and Voyages of Christopher Columbus, which dramatized a confrontation with religious authorities that never occurred.

The record
The spherical Earth was established knowledge from Aristotle onward. Columbus's critics disputed his distance calculations, not the shape of the planet. Sources: Jeffrey Burton Russell, Inventing the Flat Earth (1991); David Wootton, The Invention of Science (2015).
№ 03

Medieval people believed the Earth was flat

VERDICT: FALSE — and a 19th century invention

Medieval scholars, theologians, and educated people universally accepted the spherical Earth. Dante's Divine Comedy (1320) depicts a spherical Earth explicitly. Thomas Aquinas, Roger Bacon, and virtually every major medieval philosopher accepted it. University curricula across Europe taught the spherical Earth as fact.

The myth that medieval people believed in a flat Earth was manufactured in the 19th century to make the medieval period look ignorant by contrast with Enlightenment reason. John William Draper's History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science (1874) and Andrew Dickson White's A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology (1896) are the primary culprits. Both were polemical works with clear ideological agendas.

Historian Jeffrey Burton Russell spent a career documenting that virtually no educated person in Western history after the 3rd century BC believed in a flat Earth.

The record
The spherical Earth was standard medieval teaching. The myth of medieval flat-Earth belief was created in the 1800s. Source: Jeffrey Burton Russell, Inventing the Flat Earth (1991).
№ 04

Vikings wore horned helmets

VERDICT: FALSE

No Viking-Age battle helmet with horns has ever been excavated. The horned helmets people picture come from much older finds. The most famous are the Veksø helmets, a pair of bronze ceremonial helmets discovered in a bog in Denmark in 1942 and dated to roughly 900 BC. They predate the Viking Age by some seventeen centuries and were never weapons of war. No archaeological evidence supports horned helmets as Viking military equipment.

Actual Viking helmets were simple rounded iron caps, sometimes with a nose guard. The Gjermundbu helmet, the only complete Viking battle helmet ever found, is a plain rounded dome with an eye guard. Horns would have been actively dangerous in combat. A sword blow to a horn could wrench the neck.

The horned helmet image was popularized by 19th-century Romantic artists, particularly Carl Emil Doepler's costumes for the first production of Wagner's Ring Cycle in 1876. Hollywood cemented it. The image has no basis in the archaeological or textual record of the Viking Age.

The record
No horned helmets in Viking military archaeology. The Gjermundbu helmet (c. 970 AD) is the only complete Viking battle helmet found, and it has no horns. The horned helmets in our imagination come from Bronze Age finds like the Danish Veksø helmets (c. 900 BC). Sources: National Museum of Denmark; Neil Price, The Viking Way (2019).
№ 05

Einstein failed mathematics at school

VERDICT: FALSE

Einstein excelled at mathematics throughout his education. In his Swiss school leaving certificate, he received the highest possible marks in mathematics and physics. He taught himself calculus at 12 and had mastered integral and differential calculus before he was 15.

The myth originates from a grading system inversion. In the Swiss educational system, 6 is the highest mark and 1 is the lowest, the reverse of the German system. Early biographers saw Einstein's Swiss grades and misread a 6 as a failing mark. The error was perpetuated unchecked for decades.

Einstein did fail his entrance exam to the ETH Zurich polytechnic in 1895, but he was two years younger than the typical applicant and scored very high in mathematics and physics. He was admitted the following year after completing his schooling.

The record
Einstein's Swiss Matura certificate shows top marks in maths and physics. The myth arose from a misreading of the Swiss grading scale. Source: Abraham Pais, Subtle is the Lord: The Science and the Life of Albert Einstein (1982).
№ 06

The Great Wall of China is visible from space

VERDICT: FALSE

The Great Wall is extraordinarily long but very narrow, typically 4 to 5 meters wide. From the International Space Station at around 400 kilometers altitude, the unaided human eye can barely resolve features on the order of 100 meters across. The Wall is roughly twenty times too thin for that, and its color blends almost perfectly into the surrounding terrain. Length does not help. The Wall is too narrow and too well camouflaged to be picked out by the naked eye from orbit.

Chinese astronaut Yang Liwei, the first Chinese person in space, specifically looked for the Wall during his 2003 mission and could not see it. NASA has confirmed that the Wall is not visible from orbit with the naked eye under normal conditions, though it has been photographed with zoom lenses from the International Space Station under specific lighting conditions.

The claim appears to have been made as early as 1932 in Ripley's Believe It or Not, decades before anyone had been to space to verify it. It was repeated so often it became assumed fact.

The record
Chinese astronaut Yang Liwei could not see the Wall from orbit (2003). NASA confirms it is not visible to the naked eye from space. Source: NASA Earth Observatory; Chinese National Space Administration mission reports.
№ 07

The Magna Carta established democracy and rights for the common people

VERDICT: SIGNIFICANTLY OVERSTATED

The Magna Carta of 1215 was primarily a feudal document negotiated between King John and a group of rebellious barons who wanted to limit royal power over themselves. Most of its 63 clauses concerned specific grievances of the nobility: inheritance rights, feudal dues, forest laws, and the treatment of debtors.

However, two clauses, principally Clause 39, did contain language with broader application: "No free man shall be seized, imprisoned, dispossessed, outlawed, exiled or ruined in any way, nor in any way proceeded against, except by the lawful judgement of his peers and the law of the land." The phrase "free man" excluded serfs, who were the majority of the population.

The Magna Carta's importance as a foundation of constitutional liberty was largely a later construction, particularly by 17th-century parliamentarians who needed historical precedent for their conflicts with the Crown. Its transformation into a symbol of popular rights happened centuries after it was signed, through selective emphasis and creative interpretation.

The record
A baronial document primarily protecting noble privileges. Its reinterpretation as a popular charter of liberties developed mainly in the 17th century. Source: David Carpenter, Magna Carta (2015); Holt, Magna Carta (1992).
№ 08

Gladiators almost always fought to the death

VERDICT: FALSE

Gladiators were expensive professional athletes. They were trained, fed, housed, and given medical care by their owners and schools (the ludi). A skilled gladiator represented a significant financial investment. Letting them die in every bout would have been economically ruinous.

Archaeological evidence from gladiatorial graveyards, particularly the remarkable find at Ephesus in 1993, shows that gladiators typically suffered injuries consistent with non-lethal combat: healed fractures, blade wounds that missed vital organs, and evidence of surgical intervention. Many gladiators fought multiple times across careers spanning years.

The thumbs-up / thumbs-down gesture is also almost certainly wrong. Ancient sources use the Latin pollice verso ("turned thumb") but do not specify direction. The Hollywood version was popularized by Jean-Léon Gérôme's 1872 painting Pollice Verso, which the director Ridley Scott cited as the inspiration for Gladiator.

The record
Skeletal analysis at Ephesus shows gladiators received medical care and survived multiple fights. Death rate per bout estimated at 1 in 5 at peak periods. Source: Fabian Kanz & Karl Grossschmidt, Head Injuries of Roman Gladiators (2006).
№ 09

People in the Middle Ages had an average life expectancy of 35

VERDICT: MISLEADING — a statistical illusion

The figure is technically accurate as a mean average, but it is almost entirely driven by catastrophic child mortality. In medieval Europe, roughly a third of children died before their fifth birthday from disease, malnutrition, and infection. This drags the average down dramatically.

A medieval person who survived to age 21 could reasonably expect to live into their 50s or 60s. Records from medieval English noble families (admittedly better-documented and better-fed than peasants) show that men who reached 21 lived on average to 64. Chaucer lived to approximately 60. Edward I to 68. Eleanor of Aquitaine to around 82.

This matters because the "35 average" leads people to assume medieval adults were aged and worn out at 40, that marriage at 14 was normal for adults (it was rare and remarked upon), and that medieval people had no concept of old age. None of these are accurate.

The record
Life expectancy at birth ~35 due to child mortality. Life expectancy at age 21 was approximately 40–50 additional years. Source: Josiah Russell, British Medieval Population (1948); Rosenthal, Old Age in Late Medieval England (1996).
